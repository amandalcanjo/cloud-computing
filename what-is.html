What is cloud computing?

Cloud Computing is the on-demand availability of computer system resources, especially data storage (cloud storage) and computing power, without direct active management by the user. Large clouds often have functions distributed over multiple locations, each location being a data center.

Cloud computing relies on sharing of resources to achieve coherence and economies of scale, typically using a "pay-as-you-go" model which can help in reducing capital expenses but may also lead to unexpected operating expenses for unaware users.

History

In the 1960s, John McCarthy, considered a pioneer in the development of technology related to artificial intelligence, discussed the concept of "time-sharing computing". The idea of ​​simultaneous access, where the same computer could be used by more than one user to carry out tasks revolved around a single objective, to take advantage of the time between each process and to reduce operational and material costs for an organization. This concept was named “Utility Computing”.

The “Utility Computing” or cloud computing at the time was only possible due to the invention of ARPANET, a discovery that made it possible to expand the sharing of information and which gained global proportion.

ARPANET went through several evolutions and became what we know today as the internet. In 1990 Andy Hertzfeld and Bill Atkinson, two of the engineers at Apple Macintosh, founded General Magic and created the Telescrispt platform.
General Magic's CEO said the new artificial technology would allow him to send emails, shop and keep track of inventory, among many other features.

In 1997, the term Cloud Computing was used for the first time in an academic lecture by Ramnath Chellappa, the concept is based on the delivery of computing through a service, allowing access to data through the network. But this term became popular only in 2006 when Amazon made available in its product portfolio Amazon Web Services (AWS).
